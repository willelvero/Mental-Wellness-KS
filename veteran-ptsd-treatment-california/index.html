Levels of care

When veteran PTSD needs a higher level of care

Outpatient therapy can help many veterans with PTSD. A higher level of care may be appropriate when trauma symptoms are disrupting sleep, safety, relationships, work, substance use, emotional regulation, or the ability to function at home.

Residential PTSD treatment, PHP, or IOP may be appropriate when a veteran or active-duty service member is experiencing recurring nightmares, flashbacks, intrusive memories, severe avoidance, hypervigilance, anger, emotional numbness, panic, depression, substance use to cope, difficulty maintaining relationships, recent hospitalization, or limited progress with weekly outpatient therapy.

For many veterans and families, the goal is not only to reduce symptoms. It is to create enough structure, clinical support, trauma-focused therapy, psychiatric care, and family education to help life after service become more stable and manageable.

If you or a veteran you love is in immediate danger, call 988 and press 1 for the Veterans Crisis Line, call 911, or go to the nearest emergency room. Mental Wellness KS is not an emergency crisis response service.

Trauma therapy

Evidence-based PTSD therapies: CPT, PE, and EMDR

PTSD treatment works best when it uses therapies designed specifically for trauma. At Mental Wellness KS, veteran PTSD treatment may include Cognitive Processing Therapy, Prolonged Exposure, and EMDR when clinically appropriate.

CPT

Cognitive Processing Therapy

CPT is a structured trauma-focused therapy that helps clients identify and work through painful beliefs about themselves, others, and the world that formed during or after trauma. It may be especially helpful for guilt, shame, self-blame, and moral injury.

PE

Prolonged Exposure

PE is an evidence-based exposure therapy for PTSD that helps clients gradually approach trauma memories and avoided situations in a clinically guided way. The goal is to reduce avoidance and help the brain learn that reminders of trauma are not the same as present danger.

EMDR

Eye Movement Desensitization and Reprocessing

EMDR uses bilateral stimulation while the client focuses on traumatic memories, helping the brain process and store those memories differently. EMDR may be helpful for veterans who struggle to talk through trauma in detail, or who feel stuck in specific memories, images, or body responses.

The clinical team helps determine which trauma therapy is appropriate based on the client's symptoms, history, readiness, and treatment goals. CPT, PE, and EMDR are described here as evidence-based trauma therapies, and outcomes vary from person to person.

Forms of military trauma

Combat PTSD, MST, and moral injury

Veteran PTSD can come from many forms of trauma. Some veterans know exactly which experience changed them. Others only recognize the symptoms years later.

Combat PTSD

Combat PTSD may develop after direct combat exposure, witnessing injury or death, surviving explosions or attacks, repeated high-threat deployments, or prolonged exposure to danger.

Military sexual trauma

Military sexual trauma, also called MST, includes sexual assault or sexual harassment experienced during military service. MST can affect veterans and service members of any gender and may lead to PTSD, depression, anxiety, shame, isolation, or difficulty trusting others.

Moral injury

Moral injury can occur after witnessing, participating in, or being unable to prevent events that violate deeply held beliefs. Moral injury is not the same as PTSD, but it often appears alongside PTSD and may involve guilt, shame, grief, anger, or spiritual distress.

Symptoms

PTSD symptoms veterans often experience after service

PTSD symptoms can show up long after the traumatic event has ended. For veterans, symptoms may be mistaken for anger, isolation, drinking, work stress, relationship problems, or simply having a hard time adjusting after service.

Re-experiencing symptoms

  • Intrusive memories
  • Flashbacks
  • Nightmares
  • Physical reactions to reminders
  • Feeling like the trauma is happening again

Avoidance symptoms

  • Avoiding people, places, conversations, or activities
  • Staying busy to avoid thinking
  • Avoiding crowds, driving, news, fireworks, or reminders of service
  • Pulling away from family or friends

Mood and thinking symptoms

  • Guilt, shame, or self-blame
  • Feeling numb or detached
  • Loss of interest in things that used to matter
  • Negative beliefs about yourself, others, or the world
  • Difficulty feeling close to loved ones

Arousal and reactivity symptoms

  • Hypervigilance
  • Anger or irritability
  • Poor sleep
  • Exaggerated startle response
  • Difficulty concentrating
  • Feeling constantly on guard

Psychiatric care

Psychiatric care, sleep support, and medication management

Many veterans entering PTSD treatment are also struggling with sleep problems, nightmares, depression, anxiety, panic, anger, or substance use. Psychiatric care can help support the broader trauma treatment plan when medication is clinically appropriate.

At Mental Wellness KS, clients receive psychiatric evaluation and ongoing medication support based on their symptoms, history, prior medication response, medical considerations, and treatment goals.

Medication is not a replacement for trauma-focused therapy, but it may help reduce symptom intensity, improve sleep, support mood stability, and improve participation in treatment when clinically appropriate.

This page is general information, not prescribing advice, and does not recommend specific medications or doses. Psychiatric care at Mental Wellness KS is overseen by our Medical Director, Dr. Christian Small, MD, a board-certified psychiatrist who continues to practice at the VA in La Jolla.

Co-occurring conditions

Co-occurring conditions treated alongside PTSD

Veteran PTSD rarely appears in isolation. Many veterans and active-duty service members also struggle with depression, anxiety, substance use, sleep problems, traumatic brain injury symptoms, chronic pain, or suicidal thoughts. Mental Wellness KS screens for and treats co-occurring concerns as part of one coordinated plan when clinically appropriate.

Depression

Many veterans with PTSD also experience depression. The conditions share symptoms such as low energy, sleep disruption, and loss of interest, and addressing them together tends to support better outcomes.

Anxiety and panic

Generalized anxiety, panic attacks, and social withdrawal often accompany PTSD. As trauma-focused therapy progresses, these symptoms frequently ease alongside the PTSD itself.

Substance use concerns

Alcohol and other substances are commonly used to manage trauma symptoms. We screen for substance use on admission and treat it alongside PTSD when clinically appropriate.

Sleep problems and nightmares

Insomnia and trauma-related nightmares often persist after other symptoms improve. Sleep is addressed directly as part of the treatment plan.

Traumatic brain injury symptoms

TBI symptoms such as memory difficulty, concentration problems, and irritability can overlap with PTSD. They are assessed so each concern can be understood and addressed clearly.

Suicidal thoughts and safety planning

We screen for suicidal thoughts throughout treatment and build a safety plan with every client. If you are in immediate danger, call 988 and press 1 for the Veterans Crisis Line.

Chronic pain

Chronic pain frequently accompanies service-connected injuries. Complex medical and pain concerns are reviewed on a case-by-case basis to determine whether our program is the right fit.

If medical detox is needed, our team can help coordinate appropriate care before or alongside mental health treatment when clinically appropriate.

Family support

Family support and rebuilding trust at home

PTSD affects more than the person who lived through the trauma. Partners, parents, children, and close friends may feel confused, scared, shut out, or unsure how to help without making things worse.

At Mental Wellness KS, family support and education are included when clinically appropriate. Families can learn how PTSD symptoms work, how trauma affects communication and trust, how to support treatment participation, and how to prepare for life after residential care, PHP, or IOP.

If you are not sure what to say to a veteran you love, these are honest, low-pressure ways to start.

  • I can see something has been weighing on you.
  • You do not have to explain everything right now.
  • I am here, and I want to understand what support actually helps.
  • Would it help if I made the call with you?
  • You do not have to handle this alone.

Common questions

Frequently asked questions about veteran PTSD treatment

What is veteran PTSD?

Veteran PTSD is post-traumatic stress disorder that develops in veterans or active-duty service members after exposure to a traumatic event connected to military service. Common causes include combat exposure, military sexual trauma, and moral injury. Symptoms can include intrusive memories, nightmares, hypervigilance, avoidance, and emotional numbness. PTSD is a recognized clinical condition, and trauma-focused treatment can help reduce its impact.

What is the difference between PTSD and combat stress?

Combat stress is a common short-term reaction to the demands of deployment and combat, and it often eases after a service member returns to safety. PTSD is a clinical diagnosis involving symptoms such as intrusive memories, avoidance, negative changes in mood, and hyperarousal that last at least a month and interfere with daily life. Combat stress that does not resolve, or that returns later, can be one path toward PTSD.

Can non-combat veterans have PTSD?

Yes. PTSD can develop after any traumatic experience, not only direct combat. For service members, that can include military sexual trauma, training accidents, serious injury, witnessing death, or sustained high-threat deployments. The diagnosis is the same. What varies is the source of the trauma.

What therapies work for veteran PTSD?

Trauma-focused therapies have the strongest evidence base for PTSD. Mental Wellness KS uses Cognitive Processing Therapy (CPT), Prolonged Exposure (PE), and EMDR when clinically appropriate, alongside psychiatric care, group therapy, and family support. The clinical team helps determine which approach fits a veteran's symptoms, history, readiness, and treatment goals.

Do you offer inpatient PTSD treatment for veterans?

Many people use the word “inpatient” when searching for a higher level of mental health care. Mental Wellness KS offers residential PTSD treatment, PHP, and IOP for veterans and active-duty service members. If someone is in immediate danger, unable to stay safe, or requires acute medical stabilization, a hospital may be the appropriate first step. If they are medically stable but need structured trauma treatment, residential care may be a better fit.

How long does residential PTSD treatment last?

A typical residential PTSD stay at Mental Wellness KS is 30 to 45 days, often followed by PHP and IOP as a veteran steps down. Length of care varies by individual needs, symptoms, and progress, and the clinical team reviews the treatment plan regularly.

Will TRICARE or TriWest cover PTSD treatment?

Mental Wellness KS works with TRICARE and TriWest for eligible active-duty service members, retirees, family members, and certain veteran categories. Coverage may include residential treatment, PHP, and IOP when medically necessary. Coverage varies by plan, benefits, and level of care, and our admissions team can verify benefits at no cost.

Can VA Community Care cover PTSD treatment?

Some veterans may be eligible for care through VA Community Care when the VA cannot provide timely or geographically accessible treatment that meets their clinical needs. Eligibility and authorization are determined by the VA. Our admissions team can help discuss next steps and coordinate referral requirements when appropriate.

Will going to residential PTSD treatment affect my VA disability rating?

Treatment participation does not automatically change a VA disability rating. However, VA disability questions are legal and administrative matters, and each situation may be different. Veterans should speak with the VA, a Veterans Service Officer, or another qualified benefits professional for guidance about their specific claim or rating.

What if I am also struggling with alcohol or substance use?

PTSD and substance use often appear together in veterans, and treating both at the same time is the approach with the strongest evidence. Mental Wellness KS screens for substance use on admission. If medical detox is needed, our team can help coordinate appropriate care before or alongside PTSD treatment when clinically appropriate.

Do I have to talk about every detail of what happened?

Trauma therapy is structured and clinically guided. Some therapies involve more direct discussion of the trauma than others. The clinical team helps determine which approach fits the client's symptoms, readiness, history, and treatment goals.

What if I am in crisis right now?

If you are in immediate crisis, call 988 and press 1 for the Veterans Crisis Line, call 911, or go to the nearest emergency room. Mental Wellness KS is not an emergency crisis response service. If you are medically stable and need ongoing residential, PHP, or IOP care after crisis stabilization, our admissions team can help discuss next steps.
