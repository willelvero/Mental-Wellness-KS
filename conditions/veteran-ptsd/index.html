About PTSD

What is PTSD?

Post-traumatic stress disorder (PTSD) is a mental health condition that can develop after a person experiences or witnesses a traumatic event. Combat, military sexual trauma, life-threatening accidents, and exposure to the aftermath of violence all qualify. The trauma overwhelms the brain's normal processing system, and the body keeps responding as if the danger were still present, sometimes for years.

PTSD is not weakness, and it is not a sign that something is wrong with you. It is a clinical condition with a clear set of symptoms and a clear set of treatments that work. Most people with PTSD see major improvement with the right therapy. The hardest part is usually starting.

The veteran experience

PTSD in veterans: combat, MST, and moral injury

PTSD in veterans is structurally similar to PTSD anywhere else, but the sources of the trauma, and the culture around getting help, are specific. Three patterns come up over and over in veteran care.

Combat exposure

Direct combat, witnessing serious injury or death, surviving an IED or rocket attack, or sustained high-threat deployments. Rates of PTSD in Iraq and Afghanistan veterans range from roughly 11% to 20% depending on the study and the year examined.

Military sexual trauma (MST)

Sexual assault or sexual harassment that occurs during military service. MST is associated with very high rates of PTSD across all service eras and affects service members of every gender. The clinical work for MST-related PTSD is the same as for combat PTSD; the therapeutic stance differs.

Moral injury

Distress that follows from acting, witnessing, or failing to prevent acts that conflict with deeply held moral beliefs. Moral injury is not technically PTSD, but it often shows up alongside it. The shame and guilt components need specific clinical attention that standard PTSD therapy does not always address.

Symptoms

Symptoms of PTSD

The clinical definition of PTSD groups symptoms into four clusters. A person does not need every symptom in every cluster to be diagnosed. They do need at least some symptoms in each cluster, lasting at least a month, and affecting daily life.

01

Re-experiencing

Intrusive memories of the event, vivid flashbacks, recurring nightmares, or strong physical reactions when reminded of what happened.

02

Avoidance

Avoiding places, people, conversations, or activities that bring up reminders of the trauma. Sometimes this looks like staying inside, leaving early, or never talking about service.

03

Negative changes in mood and thinking

Persistent guilt, shame, hopelessness, or feeling cut off from other people. Loss of interest in things that used to matter. Negative beliefs about yourself, others, or the world.

04

Arousal and reactivity

Constantly on guard. Sleeping poorly. Anger that fires faster and harder than the situation calls for. An exaggerated startle response. Difficulty concentrating.

Our treatment

How we treat veteran PTSD

The three trauma-focused therapies with the strongest research base for PTSD are CPT, PE, and EMDR. All three are recommended by the VA and the Department of Defense as first-line treatment. Your primary therapist will work with you to pick the one that fits you best. Many veterans use elements of more than one across a stay.

CPT

Cognitive Processing Therapy

A 12-session, structured approach that helps you identify and change the beliefs you formed about yourself, others, and the world during or after the trauma. Especially useful for guilt and shame, and for veterans who want a clear, sequenced program.

PE

Prolonged Exposure

A structured exposure-based therapy that has you approach the memory of the trauma directly, in sessions and through homework, until the memory loses its grip. PE has the largest research base of any PTSD therapy and tends to produce fast, lasting results.

EMDR

Eye Movement Desensitization and Reprocessing

Uses guided eye movements (or other bilateral stimulation) while you bring the trauma memory to mind, so the memory can be processed and stored differently. EMDR often works for veterans who find it hard to talk through the trauma in detail.

Trauma-focused therapy at Mental Wellness KS is delivered alongside individual therapy, group work, psychiatric care, family sessions, and time outside. The therapy is the engine. The structured weeks around it are what make the therapy stick.

The impact of PTSD

How untreated veteran PTSD shapes a life after service

PTSD does not stay in the war zone. The same hyperarousal, avoidance, and intrusion patterns that kept service members alive in combat work against them at home. Untreated, the cost compounds in specific, predictable domains.

01

Marriage and family

Hypervigilance, emotional withdrawal, and irritability put significant strain on partners and children. Divorce rates are higher in veterans with untreated PTSD than in those who receive treatment. The relational repair is possible, but the underlying condition has to be treated first.

02

Work and career after service

Sleep loss, irritability, and avoidance of high-stress situations narrow career options. Veterans with untreated PTSD often underperform relative to their skills, change jobs more frequently, and have lower long-term earnings than their service peers.

03

Substance use

Alcohol, prescription pain medication, and other substances are commonly used to manage PTSD symptoms. The pattern often becomes its own diagnosis. Treating PTSD and substance use simultaneously is the only approach with strong evidence.

04

Physical health and safety

Chronic activation of the stress response raises cardiovascular risk, disrupts sleep, and accelerates aging. Veteran suicide rates are significantly elevated when PTSD is untreated, particularly when paired with depression or substance use. If you or a veteran you love is in crisis now, call 988 and press 1 for the Veterans Crisis Line.

Other conditions to know

PTSD and other conditions we treat alongside it

PTSD in veterans rarely appears on its own. We screen for and treat the most common co-occurring conditions as part of the same plan.

Traumatic brain injury (TBI)

Especially common in veterans who served in OEF/OIF. TBI symptoms (memory, concentration, irritability) overlap with PTSD symptoms and need to be separated clinically so each one can be treated.

Depression

Roughly half of veterans with PTSD also have clinical depression. The two share symptoms (sleep, energy, anhedonia), and treating both at once produces better outcomes than treating either alone.

Substance use

Alcohol and substance use often start as a way to cope with trauma symptoms. We assess on admission. For active dependence, our sister program at Ken Seeley Communities handles medical detox before or alongside the PTSD work here.

Suicidal thinking

Veteran suicide rates are significantly higher than the civilian rate. We screen for suicidal thinking continuously, build a safety plan with every client, and use Cognitive Behavioral Therapy for Suicide Prevention (CBT-SP) where indicated.

Sleep problems

Nightmares and insomnia often outlast the rest of the PTSD picture. Prazosin can help with trauma-related nightmares. CBT for insomnia (CBT-I) addresses sleep architecture itself. Both are part of the plan when needed.

Anxiety and panic

Generalized anxiety, panic attacks, and social withdrawal commonly accompany PTSD. The trauma-focused therapy work usually reduces these as the PTSD itself improves.

Medication

Medication for PTSD

Medication is not first-line for PTSD on its own, but it is often a useful part of a combined plan with therapy. Two SSRIs are FDA-approved specifically for PTSD: sertraline and paroxetine. Venlafaxine (an SNRI) has good evidence as well. These medications can take 6 to 12 weeks at a stable dose to show their full effect.

For trauma-related nightmares, prazosin is the most commonly used add-on. It works at the level of the body's nighttime stress response and can produce noticeable change within the first few weeks. The dosing is individual and is set in cooperation with your psychiatric provider.

Benzodiazepines (alprazolam, lorazepam) are generally not recommended for PTSD treatment. Research shows they can interfere with the recovery process and carry a higher risk of dependence in veteran populations. If you arrive on a benzodiazepine, the team will help you taper carefully and find safer alternatives.

This is an overview, not prescribing advice. All medication at Mental Wellness KS is prescribed and managed by Dr. Christian Small, MD, our board-certified Medical Director, who also continues to practice at the VA in La Jolla.

Levels of care

Residential, PHP, and IOP for veteran PTSD

PTSD treatment is a phased process. The trauma work is intensive and benefits from the structure that higher levels of care provide. Most veterans move through all three, then step down to community-based outpatient care.

Residential

30 to 45 days · 24-hour care

The phase where the trauma-focused therapy (CPT, PE, EMDR) begins, paired with psychiatric care, sleep stabilization, and substance-use treatment when needed. Daily clinical contact. A small program, with veterans treated alongside civilians but with veteran-specific group programming.

PHP

6 to 12 weeks · Mon–Fri, 6 hrs/day

Continued trauma-focused therapy with the same primary therapist, plus group, family work, and ongoing psychiatric oversight. Evenings spent at home or in supported housing. The phase where the gains from residential consolidate and start being tested in ordinary life.

IOP

6 to 12 weeks · 3 hrs/day, 3 days/week

Designed to coexist with returning to work, school, or family responsibilities. The same primary therapist continues. By this point the trauma work has done its primary lift, and the focus is consolidation, relapse prevention, and the small repairs to ordinary life that PTSD has interrupted.

Common questions

Frequently asked questions about veteran PTSD treatment

What is the difference between PTSD and combat stress?

Combat stress is a normal short-term reaction to combat exposure and usually resolves within weeks after a service member returns to safety. PTSD is a clinical diagnosis that requires symptoms (intrusive memories, avoidance, negative mood changes, hyperarousal) lasting at least a month and significantly disrupting daily life. Many veterans experience combat stress without developing PTSD, but combat stress that does not resolve, or that re-activates after a delay, is one common path into PTSD.

Can non-combat veterans have PTSD?

Yes. PTSD can develop from any exposure to a traumatic event. For service members, that can include military sexual trauma (MST), training accidents, motor vehicle accidents, witnessing a death, exposure to the aftermath of violence, or sustained high-threat deployments that did not involve direct combat. The diagnosis is the same; the source of the trauma is what varies.

What therapies actually work for veteran PTSD?

The three trauma-focused therapies with the strongest research base for PTSD are Cognitive Processing Therapy (CPT), Prolonged Exposure (PE), and Eye Movement Desensitization and Reprocessing (EMDR). All three are recommended by the VA and the Department of Defense. The right one for any individual veteran depends on what fits them and their clinical picture. At Mental Wellness KS, primary therapists are trained in CPT, PE, and EMDR.

Will TRICARE or VA Community Care cover my treatment?

Mental Wellness KS works with TRICARE and TriWest, which are the main programs that cover non-VA care for active-duty service members, family members, and veterans referred through the VA Community Care Network. We also accept most commercial insurance. A 10-minute call to (866) 460-4462 will tell you what your specific plan covers. There is no charge for the verification.

Will going to residential treatment affect my VA disability rating?

Seeking treatment does not lower an existing disability rating. In many cases, documented treatment supports a claim by establishing a clear clinical record. We can help with the paperwork side if it would be useful, and we recommend any veteran with specific concerns talk to a VA-accredited representative or VSO before, during, or after treatment.

How long does residential PTSD treatment last?

Residential stays at Mental Wellness KS run 30 to 45 days, typically followed by PHP (6 to 12 weeks) and IOP (6 to 12 weeks). Trauma-focused therapy continues across all three levels. Most veterans need the full continuum to see lasting change. The work that begins in residential takes its strongest hold during PHP and IOP, when exposures are happening in real-world contexts.

What if I'm also struggling with alcohol or substance use?

PTSD and substance use frequently appear together in veterans. We treat both at the same time. If medical detox is needed first, we coordinate with our sister program at Ken Seeley Communities, which is set up for that level of care. After detox, you would step into the PTSD work here.

Do I have to share details about what happened to start treatment?

No. The first call is about whether we are a fit, what your insurance covers, and what admission would look like. You do not need to walk anyone through the worst day of your life on the phone. The trauma work happens with your primary therapist, on your timeline, after you have arrived and you trust the room.
