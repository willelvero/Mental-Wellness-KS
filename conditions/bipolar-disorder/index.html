The diagnostic gap

Why bipolar disorder is so often misdiagnosed.

Most adults with bipolar disorder are first treated for something else, usually depression. The reason is not careless medicine. It is the structure of the illness itself: most people reach for help during a depressive episode, when the symptoms are painful and disabling. The hypomanic phases that would clarify the picture often feel productive, energetic, or simply like finally being well. They do not prompt a phone call to a clinician.

The result is a long, frustrating loop. Antidepressants are prescribed and sometimes help, sometimes do not, sometimes destabilize. Mood episodes return. New medications are tried. Years pass. The average delay between first symptoms and an accurate bipolar diagnosis is roughly six to ten years.

The most useful question we ask new clients is not "are you depressed?" It is: "Have there ever been stretches, even brief ones, where you needed almost no sleep, your thoughts moved faster than your speech, and you felt unusually capable or invincible?" The answer to that question is often the missing piece.

Getting the diagnosis right

Bipolar I, Bipolar II, and cyclothymia.

These are three distinct clinical pictures, not one illness with different severity levels. They have different episode signatures, different treatment trajectories, and different long-term considerations. Knowing which one you have changes what works.

Bipolar I

Full manic episodes, with or without depression

Defining episode
At least one full manic episode (1 week or more, or requiring hospitalization)
Depression
Common but not required for diagnosis
Hospitalization risk
Higher; mania can include psychotic features
Treatment focus
Mood stabilization, mania prevention, sleep regulation

Bipolar II

Hypomanic episodes plus major depression

Defining episode
At least one hypomanic episode (4+ days) plus at least one major depressive episode
Depression
Usually the dominant and most disabling feature
Hospitalization risk
Lower for hypomania; depressive episodes can be severe
Treatment focus
Mood stabilization, careful antidepressant decisions, depression treatment

Cyclothymia

Chronic, lower-grade mood cycling

Defining episode
Hypomanic and depressive symptoms that don't meet full episode criteria, lasting 2 years or more
Depression
Subclinical but persistent
Hospitalization risk
Lower; risk of progression to Bipolar I or II over time
Treatment focus
Mood stabilization, monitoring for progression, lifestyle structure

The clinical vocabulary

The four mood-episode types.

Bipolar disorder is described in terms of episodes, not constant states. Most adults with bipolar spend the majority of their time outside of an episode entirely. Knowing the four episode types, and learning to recognize them early, is one of the most useful skills a client can leave treatment with.

Mania

1 week or more · may require hospitalization

Sustained elevated, expansive, or irritable mood plus marked increase in energy. Decreased need for sleep (not insomnia, no need for sleep), pressured speech, racing thoughts, grandiosity, impulsive or risky behavior, and sometimes psychotic features. Severe enough to disrupt work, relationships, and safety.

Hypomania

4 days or more · usually no hospitalization

The same constellation as mania but less intense and shorter. Often experienced as productive, energetic, or "finally well." This is the episode type most often missed, both by clients and by clinicians. Family members frequently notice it before the client does.

Depression

2 weeks or more · most common time clients seek help

Clinically indistinguishable in the moment from unipolar major depression: persistent low mood, loss of pleasure, sleep and appetite disruption, fatigue, hopelessness. The bipolar-vs-unipolar distinction is made from the history, not the current presentation. (Read more on our depression treatment page.)

Mixed features

Variable · clinically high-risk

Symptoms of mania and depression simultaneously, often the most dangerous presentation because the energy of mania is paired with the hopelessness of depression. Mixed episodes carry elevated risk for self-harm and require careful clinical management.

A clinical caution

Why standard depression medications can make bipolar worse.

One of the most important reasons accurate bipolar diagnosis matters is what happens when it is missed. Antidepressants used alone, without a mood stabilizer, can trigger a manic or hypomanic episode, accelerate the cycling between episodes, or worsen mixed features in adults with bipolar disorder. This is sometimes called an antidepressant-induced switch, and it is one of the most common ways bipolar finally becomes visible: the depression medication that worked for a friend produces a strange, accelerated, irritable elevation in a person who turns out to have bipolar II.

This does not mean antidepressants are off the table for bipolar treatment. It means they are used carefully, in specific clinical situations, almost always alongside a mood stabilizer, and with daily psychiatric supervision in the early stages. For clients who arrive at Mental Wellness KS carrying years of antidepressant trials that haven't quite worked, the first job is often a fresh psychiatric reassessment, not a sixth medication.

The medication class that matters most

Mood stabilizers, and what they actually do.

Bipolar disorder is a chronic medical condition. Like other chronic conditions, the foundation of long-term stability is medication that addresses the underlying biology. For bipolar, that medication class is mood stabilizers, and the specific choice matters.

Lithium

The original mood stabilizer. Strong evidence for preventing both manic and depressive episodes; the most consistently effective option for many adults with Bipolar I. Requires periodic blood-level monitoring.

Valproate (Depakote)

Originally an anti-seizure medication, also effective for acute mania and mood stabilization. Often used when lithium is not the right fit.

Lamotrigine

Particularly effective for the depressive side of Bipolar II. Slower titration, but well-tolerated long-term for many adults.

Atypical antipsychotics

Lurasidone, quetiapine, olanzapine, and others have FDA approval for various phases of bipolar. Used both for acute episodes and for ongoing mood stabilization, often in combination with another stabilizer.

Antidepressants

Used selectively for the depressive phase of Bipolar I and II, almost always alongside a mood stabilizer, and never as a standalone treatment.

This is an overview, not prescribing advice. Every regimen at Mental Wellness KS is set and adjusted by Dr. Christian Small, MD, our board-certified Medical Director, based on the individual clinical picture.

How treatment is structured

How we treat bipolar disorder at Mental Wellness KS.

Bipolar treatment is the place where medication and therapy have to work as a single system. The psychiatric work happens daily during residential and PHP, with Dr. Small directly involved. The therapy work runs in parallel, five days a week. The two cannot live in different buildings on different schedules.

The evidence-based therapies we use most often for bipolar disorder are not the same as those for anxiety. The four that carry the strongest research base are:

Psychoeducation
Teaching clients and families the clinical picture of bipolar disorder, how to recognize early warning signs, and what predicts relapse. Sounds basic. Has more research evidence for preventing future episodes than almost anything else we do.
Family-Focused Therapy (FFT)
A structured, bipolar-specific family therapy with strong evidence for reducing relapse rates. Brings family members into the treatment as informed partners, not bystanders.
Interpersonal & Social Rhythm Therapy (IPSRT)
Built specifically for bipolar disorder. Focuses on stabilizing daily routines (sleep, meals, social contact, light exposure) because rhythm disruption is one of the most reliable predictors of a coming episode.
CBT, DBT & mood charting
Cognitive-behavioral and DBT skills work, paired with daily mood tracking that gives clients (and the clinical team) early visibility into shifts before they become full episodes.

The level-of-care continuum, residential (30 to 45 days), PHP (6 to 12 weeks), and IOP (6 to 12 weeks), is the structure inside which all of this happens. Most clients move through all three.

The lifestyle piece that actually matters

Sleep, light, and the daily rhythm.

Bipolar disorder is the rare mental-health condition where lifestyle interventions are not soft support, they are part of the medicine. The reason is biological: bipolar mood episodes are strongly tied to circadian and social rhythms, and the single most reliable trigger for a manic episode is sleep loss.

01

Sleep, treated as medicine

A fixed sleep window, protected. We work with clients to establish a consistent bedtime and wake time, with the same level of clinical attention given to medication. One bad night is not a crisis. A pattern is.

02

Light exposure

Morning sunlight, evening dimming. Bright light at the right time of day helps anchor circadian rhythm; bright light at the wrong time can destabilize. Palm Springs is one of the best places in the country to practice this.

03

Social rhythm

Meals, social contact, and physical activity on a roughly consistent schedule. IPSRT (above) is the clinical framework. The lived practice is just: the same kind of week, most weeks.

04

Substance use, carefully

Alcohol and stimulants are common destabilizers for bipolar. So is high-dose caffeine close to bedtime. We address substance use as part of the relapse-prevention plan, not as a moral question.

For the family

When a loved one is in a manic phase, or a depressive phase.

The hardest part of being close to someone with bipolar disorder is that the person you are trying to help shows up differently in different episodes. The strategies that help during mania are not the strategies that help during depression. A few practical distinctions:

If they are in a manic or hypomanic phase

  • Do not argue with grandiose plans in the moment. Engagement fuels the episode; you can revisit when the elevation settles.
  • Protect what is protectable: shared finances, car keys, access to substances. This is care, not control.
  • Watch for sleep loss. Three or four nights of minimal sleep is often the early sign of escalation.
  • Call the clinical team. If your loved one is in treatment, the psychiatric provider needs to know about an episode in motion, ideally the same day.
  • If safety is in question, call 988 or 911. Acute mania can include psychotic features and impaired judgment that warrant emergency care.

If they are in a depressive phase

  • Lead with concern, not advice. "I have been worried about you" lands better than "you need help."
  • Lower the bar. Small, concrete next steps ("can we make one phone call together this week") work; large ones do not.
  • Hold the schedule. Help maintain sleep, meals, and basic routine; rhythm itself is treatment.
  • Ask directly about suicidal thinking. The question does not put the thought there. It opens the door.
  • If your loved one is in immediate danger, call 988 or go to the nearest emergency room.

Common questions

Frequently asked questions about bipolar treatment

What is the difference between Bipolar I and Bipolar II?

Bipolar I requires at least one full manic episode (lasting a week or more, or requiring hospitalization). Bipolar II involves at least one hypomanic episode (a less intense, shorter elevation in mood) plus at least one major depressive episode, but never a full manic episode. The depressive episodes in Bipolar II are often more frequent and more disabling than the hypomanic ones, which is why Bipolar II is so often misdiagnosed as recurrent depression.

Can antidepressants make bipolar disorder worse?

In some cases, yes. Antidepressants used without a mood stabilizer can trigger a manic or hypomanic switch, accelerate cycling, or worsen mixed features in adults with bipolar disorder. This is one of the reasons accurate diagnosis matters so much. At Mental Wellness KS, Dr. Christian Small, MD, reviews every regimen on admission and adjusts the approach when a bipolar diagnosis changes the calculus.

Why is bipolar disorder so often misdiagnosed as depression?

Most adults with Bipolar II seek treatment during a depressive episode. The hypomanic phases often feel productive, energetic, or "finally normal," so they rarely prompt a phone call to a clinician. The average delay between first symptoms and an accurate bipolar diagnosis is six to ten years, almost always spent on a depression treatment that does not fully fit.

What medications are used to treat bipolar disorder?

Mood stabilizers are the foundation: lithium, valproate, lamotrigine, and certain atypical antipsychotics (lurasidone, quetiapine, others) all have evidence for different presentations. Antidepressants are used selectively and almost always alongside a mood stabilizer. The specific regimen is highly individual and is set by Dr. Christian Small, MD, our board-certified Medical Director, during the first days of treatment.

How does residential treatment for bipolar disorder work?

Residential is the right level when a manic, mixed, or severely depressive episode needs 24-hour observation, when a medication regimen needs daily psychiatric supervision while it stabilizes, or when sleep, safety, and structure cannot be reliably maintained at home. A typical stay is 30 to 45 days, followed by PHP and IOP. Most clients leave residential with a working medication regimen, a relapse-prevention plan, and a clear picture of their personal early-warning signs.

Most commercial plans cover residential, PHP, and IOP for bipolar disorder when medically necessary. Can bipolar disorder be cured?

Bipolar disorder is a chronic condition, not a curable one, but it is highly treatable. With the right combination of mood stabilization, evidence-based therapy, structure, and self-monitoring, most adults with bipolar disorder can achieve long stretches of stability and live full, productive lives. The goal of treatment is sustained stability, not cure.

What if my loved one is in a manic episode right now?

If your loved one is in immediate danger, has lost contact with reality, or cannot be kept safe, call 988 or go to the nearest emergency room. For active mania that does not yet require emergency care, call (866) 460-4462. We can help you determine whether residential admission is the right next step and walk you through what that admission would look like.
