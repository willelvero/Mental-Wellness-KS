About self-harm

What is self-harm?

Self-harm, also called nonsuicidal self-injury (NSSI), is the deliberate harming of one's own body without the intent to end one's life. Cutting is the most common form, but burning, scratching, hitting oneself, and interfering with wound healing all count. The clinical name is intentionally precise: nonsuicidal. Most people who self-injure are not trying to die. They are trying to manage a feeling they have no other tool for.

That distinction is important, but it is not the whole picture. Self-injury and suicidal thinking can live in the same person, and the behavior itself raises the risk of accidental serious injury over time. Both are taken seriously. Both deserve real treatment, not lectures and not silence.

The function

Why people self-harm

Self-harm always does something for the person doing it. That sounds counterintuitive, but it is the most important sentence in this whole field. If the behavior did nothing, it would not be so hard to stop. Understanding what the behavior is doing for you is the first step toward replacing it with something that does the same job without the wound.

01

Emotion regulation

The most common function. A feeling is so big and so unbearable that the body's response to physical pain becomes a way of bringing the volume down. The injury produces a brief, real change in nervous-system state. Then the feeling rushes back, and so does the urge.

02

A sense of control

When something else in life feels uncontrollable, an injury is one thing the person can decide on, time, and end. It is one of the few choices that feels fully their own. The relief comes from the choosing, not just the cut.

03

Communicating what cannot be said

For some people, words for the inner state have never been there. The wound becomes a way of making the pain visible, to oneself or to someone close. This pattern often shows up in people with trauma history where speaking was unsafe.

04

Self-punishment

When shame is the dominant feeling, the injury can feel deserved. Therapy here is less about stopping the cutting at the surface and more about working on the belief underneath: that the person is bad and needs to be punished.

Warning signs

Signs to watch for, in yourself or someone you love

Self-harm is often hidden, and people who self-injure are often skilled at the hiding. These are the patterns that most often surface first. None of them are proof on their own; together, they are a picture worth a careful conversation.

What you might see

  • Long sleeves and pants in warm weather, even at home
  • Frequent unexplained scratches, burns, or bruises, often in clusters or patterns
  • Scars that follow lines, especially on forearms, thighs, or stomach
  • Bandages, wrist coverings, or bracelets that never come off
  • Blood on clothing or in laundry that is not explained
  • Sharp objects kept in unusual places: bedroom, bathroom, bag, car

What you might notice in their behavior

  • Long stretches of time alone behind a closed door, especially in the bathroom
  • Sudden retreat to bedroom after an emotional conversation or argument
  • A flat, distant mood that lifts after the retreat, in a way that does not quite track
  • Avoidance of pools, beaches, locker rooms, or anywhere the skin is exposed
  • Strong reactions to being touched on a covered area
  • Statements that imply the person feels deserving of pain or unworthy of care

Our treatment

How we treat self-harm

Self-harm is one of the few mental-health pictures where the field has a clear, evidence-backed first-line treatment. That treatment is dialectical behavior therapy, developed by Marsha Linehan in the 1980s specifically for people who self-injure. Decades of research have not unseated it. At Mental Wellness KS, DBT is the spine; everything else is built around it.

Dialectical Behavior Therapy (DBT)
Four skill areas taught in group, applied in individual therapy, practiced between sessions: mindfulness, distress tolerance, emotion regulation, and interpersonal effectiveness. DBT is the treatment with the strongest evidence base for reducing self-injury and is the heart of the self-harm program here.
Trauma-focused therapy (EMDR, CPT)
When trauma history is part of the picture, which it often is, trauma-focused work happens alongside the DBT, not after it. EMDR and Cognitive Processing Therapy are both available.
Psychiatric care
Our Medical Director evaluates and treats the conditions underneath the self-harm. Depression, anxiety, PTSD, bipolar disorder, and mood instability all show up commonly. Treating them well usually brings the self-injury urge down.
Family work
Where the client consents, family sessions teach the people closest to them how to respond when the urge comes back, how to talk about the topic without inflaming it, and how to take care of themselves in the process.

Levels of care

Residential, PHP, and IOP for self-harm

Self-harm treatment is a step-down sequence, not a single level. Most clients start at the level of care that matches the current safety picture and then move down as the urge eases and the skills hold. Each step is its own admission and its own authorization.

Residential

30 to 45 days

Residential self-harm treatment

24-hour staffing, private room, structured daily DBT skills group, individual therapy several times a week, and daily psychiatric oversight. The right level when self-harm is current and ongoing, when prior outpatient treatment has not held, or when a co-occurring condition (depression, PTSD, bipolar) is driving the urge and needs to be stabilized first.

PHP

6 to 12 weeks

Partial Hospitalization Program

Six hours a day, weekdays, evenings spent at home or in supported housing. The natural step down from residential. DBT skills group continues. Individual therapy continues. Medication management continues. The difference is that the client begins re-engaging with life outside the program in the evenings.

IOP

6 to 12 weeks

Intensive Outpatient Program

Three hours a day, three days a week. Designed to coexist with returning to work, school, or family. By this point, the DBT skills are familiar and the work is about maintaining them under real-world load. Most clients step down to community-based outpatient therapy after IOP.

Other conditions to know

Conditions that often appear with self-harm

Self-harm rarely shows up on its own. In most adults, it is sitting on top of one or more underlying conditions that have not been adequately treated. Identifying and treating what is underneath is often the work that makes the urge to self-injure quiet down. We screen for the most common patterns at intake.

Depression

The most common condition underneath adult self-harm. The hopelessness and emotional flatness of depression often produce the kind of unbearable internal state that the wound is trying to interrupt. Treating the depression well, with therapy and medication, frequently quiets the self-injury urge as the mood lifts.

Anxiety

Generalized anxiety, panic, and social anxiety all show up at higher rates in adults who self-injure. The wound can function as a brief reset of an overwhelmed nervous system. Working on the underlying anxiety with CBT and, where appropriate, medication, gives the body a slower, gentler way down.

PTSD and trauma history

A history of trauma is one of the strongest predictors of self-injury in adulthood. The pattern often connects directly to the trauma: a way of speaking what words cannot, or a way of feeling something other than numb. Trauma-focused therapy (EMDR, CPT) is part of the plan when this picture is present.

Bipolar disorder

Mixed-features episodes, where the energy of mania meets the hopelessness of depression, carry elevated risk for self-injury and require careful clinical management. Getting the mood stabilizer regimen right is foundational; the self-harm urge frequently eases once the underlying mood is steadier.

Medication

Medication and self-harm

There is no medication approved specifically for self-injury. The medication conversation in this program is always about the condition underneath: depression, anxiety, PTSD, mood instability, or sometimes bipolar disorder. When those are treated well, the urge to self-harm often quiets significantly.

SSRIs and SNRIs are the most common medications when depression or anxiety is the driver. Mood stabilizers are used when the picture is more about emotional intensity and reactivity. Benzodiazepines are used cautiously, because they can blunt the skills work and carry dependence risk.

All medication at Mental Wellness KS is prescribed and managed by our board-certified Medical Director. The goal is the smallest effective regimen, not the largest one.

Insurance coverage

Will insurance cover treatment, and who will know?

Two questions come up almost every time someone calls about self-harm care: will my insurance cover this, and who finds out what. Both have clear answers.

Will my insurance cover residential treatment for self-injury?

In most cases, yes. Mental-health residential, PHP, and IOP are covered by most commercial plans when medically necessary. Self-harm with a current safety picture almost always meets that bar. We verify benefits and authorization in a 10-minute call before you commit to anything.

Will my insurance company know what I did?

Insurers receive diagnostic codes and authorization information. They do not get therapy notes. The codes used for self-injury treatment are part of routine mental-health billing. The carrier knows the level of care; they do not get the contents of your sessions.

Does my family have to know?

No. Adults control their own information. Mental Wellness KS does not contact family without your written release. We will encourage family involvement when it will help your recovery, but the timing and whether it happens at all are your decisions.

In-network with United Healthcare, United Behavioral Health, Cigna, Aetna, Blue Cross Blue Shield, TRICARE, TriWest, Anthem, Meritain, Premera, Medica, Regence, and others. Out-of-network also accepted.

Common questions

Frequently asked questions about self-harm treatment

Is self-harm the same as a suicide attempt?

No. Nonsuicidal self-injury is the deliberate harming of one's own body without the intent to die. Most people who self-injure are trying to manage an unbearable feeling, not end their life. That said, self-injury and suicidal thinking can exist in the same person, and self-injury increases the risk of accidental serious injury. Both are taken seriously at Mental Wellness KS, and the clinical team screens carefully for both at intake.

Will my insurance company know what I did?

Insurance carriers receive diagnostic codes and authorization information, not therapy notes. The codes used for self-injury treatment are part of standard mental-health billing. Insurers do not get a play-by-play of what you did or said in session. The team can walk you through exactly what your specific plan will see in a 10-minute call.

Do I have to tell my family why I'm coming to treatment?

No. You are an adult, and you control what information goes to whom. Mental Wellness KS does not contact family members without your written release. The clinical team will encourage family involvement where it will help your recovery, but the timing, the wording, and whether it happens at all are your choices to make.

What kind of therapy works best for self-harm?

Dialectical behavior therapy, or DBT, has the strongest evidence base for reducing self-injury. DBT was developed specifically for self-injuring clients and works by teaching four skills areas: mindfulness, distress tolerance, emotion regulation, and interpersonal effectiveness. CBT and trauma-focused therapies like EMDR are often added when trauma history or other conditions are part of the picture.

Are there medications for self-harm?

There are no medications approved specifically for self-injury. Medication is used when a co-occurring condition is driving the self-harm, most often depression, anxiety, PTSD, or mood instability. Treating the underlying condition often reduces the self-harm urge significantly. Our Medical Director sets and adjusts all medication in residential.

Will I have to be on a 24/7 watch?

Mental Wellness KS is residential treatment, not a hospital. Clients have privacy, individual rooms, and freedom of movement on campus. We do remove sharp objects from rooms during the initial period and replace them as the clinical picture clears. The level of supervision is set by the clinical team based on the current safety picture, not by a one-size-fits-all rule.

What if I relapse during or after treatment?

Relapse is a clinical event, not a moral failure. If self-harm returns during residential, we adjust the plan, look at what the urge was responding to, and add the skills that were missing. After discharge, the aftercare conversation always includes what to do if the urge returns. A slip is information; it is not the end of the work.
