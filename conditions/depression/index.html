Different forms of depression

Common symptoms of clinical depression

Depression rarely shows up the same way twice. Some adults describe it as sadness; many do not. What unifies the diagnosis is a cluster of symptoms across mood, body, and thinking that persists for two weeks or longer and starts to interfere with daily life.

Mood & emotional

  • Persistent low, empty, or hopeless feeling
  • Loss of interest or pleasure (anhedonia)
  • Excessive guilt or feelings of worthlessness
  • Irritability or shortened temper
  • Numbness, the absence of feeling, not the presence of sadness

Physical & somatic

  • Sleep disruption, insomnia, early waking, or sleeping all day
  • Significant appetite or weight change, in either direction
  • Fatigue that is physical, not just mental
  • Psychomotor slowing or restlessness
  • Unexplained aches, pains, or digestive issues

Cognitive & behavioral

  • Difficulty concentrating, deciding, or finishing tasks
  • Memory and processing-speed changes
  • Social withdrawal, canceling, isolating, going quiet
  • Rumination, the loop of self-attack that won't switch off
  • Recurrent thoughts of death, self-harm, or suicide

What drives it

What causes depression?

Depression rarely has a single cause. Most adults we admit are carrying several risk factors layered together, some inherited, some environmental, some medical, some all three at once. Naming what is driving it is the first step toward treating it.

Biological
Family history of depression or bipolar disorder, neurotransmitter dysregulation, inflammatory factors, structural and functional differences in mood-regulating brain circuits.
Medical & hormonal
Thyroid disease, chronic pain conditions, traumatic brain injury, postpartum hormonal shifts, perimenopause, sleep disorders, vitamin deficiencies, and certain prescription medications.
Trauma & early adversity
Childhood abuse or neglect, sexual assault, combat exposure, prolonged caregiving stress, complicated bereavement. Trauma is the single most common driver in the clients we treat.
Situational & relational
Chronic occupational stress and burnout, financial crisis, loss of a primary relationship, social isolation, immigration or relocation stress, the slow accumulation of an unlivable life.
Co-occurring
Substance use, untreated anxiety, post-traumatic stress, eating disorders, ADHD. Roughly half of the adults we treat for depression carry a second active condition.

Clinical depression categories

How clinical depression takes place in adults

Diagnostic manuals divide depression into discrete categories. In practice, the more useful distinction is the pattern, how the depression came on, what tends to drive it, and what treatment that pattern responds to.

Episodic depression

Includes: Major Depressive Disorder (MDD)

Distinct episodes of significant low mood lasting weeks to months, often with a relatively well period in between. The most common pattern, and the one most adults are familiar with from clinical descriptions. Responds well to a combination of psychotherapy and properly-dosed medication.

Persistent depression

Includes: Persistent Depressive Disorder (Dysthymia), “double depression”

A chronic, lower-grade depression that has run in the background of life for two years or more. Clients often describe it as feeling like a baseline rather than an episode, “I have always been this way.” Treatment tends to be slower, more relational, and more sustained than for an acute MDD episode.

Trauma-rooted depression

Includes: Depression with co-occurring PTSD, complex PTSD, or unresolved early adversity

Depression where a discrete trauma or sustained adversity is the engine. Standard antidepressant trials often fall short here. The work is trauma-focused therapy, EMDR, Cognitive Processing Therapy, alongside the depression treatment. A significant portion of our veteran clients fit this pattern.

Hormonally-driven depression

Includes: Postpartum & Perinatal Depression, Premenstrual Dysphoric Disorder (PMDD), Seasonal Affective Disorder (SAD), perimenopausal depression

Depression that tracks closely with hormonal or circadian shifts, pregnancy and the postpartum period, menstrual cycle phase, seasonal light exposure, perimenopause. The medical workup matters here. So does treating the depression itself with full clinical rigor; “it's just hormones” is rarely the whole story.

Bipolar depression

Includes: Depressive episodes within Bipolar I or Bipolar II disorder

The depressive phase of bipolar disorder. The presentation overlaps with unipolar depression, but the treatment is different, standard antidepressants used alone can sometimes destabilize. We screen carefully on admission and treat bipolar depression as part of our broader mood-disorder programming.

Treatment-resistant depression

Includes: Any of the above that has not responded to two or more medication trials

Cuts across the patterns above. What makes it “treatment-resistant” is the response history, not the underlying form. The treatment answer is the same: a higher level of care where the psychiatric work and the therapy happen together every day, not separately once a month.

The impact of depression

How untreated depression affects daily life

Depression looks different for everyone, but it rarely stays still. Left untreated, it tends to reach into work, relationships, physical health, and the inner sense of who a person is. Recognizing where it has already shown up is often the first step toward getting help.

Work and identity

For many adults, concentration is one of the first things to slip, and productivity often follows. Work that used to feel ordinary can start to take longer and feel heavier. Over months, this can show up in performance reviews, promotion timing, and a quieter internal sense of being less competent. Many adults with untreated depression describe a private shame about the gap between what they could be doing and what they are. Not everyone experiences this the same way.

Relationships and family

For many people, depression can flatten the warmth that close relationships run on. That often looks like becoming harder to reach, harder to plan with, and less responsive to the ordinary affection that used to feel like air. Partners and children may notice the change even when they cannot name it. The strain tends to compound quietly, and the longer it sits, the more time repair can take. How much shows up at home varies from person to person.

Physical health

Depression is rarely just in the head. Sleep can be disrupted, appetite can shift in either direction, movement often falls off, and pain perception may sharpen. The combination of symptoms looks different for everyone. In adults with chronic untreated depression, the long-term risk of cardiovascular disease, diabetes, and stroke is meaningfully higher at the population level. The body tends to pay for the mental state.

Safety and suicidality

For adults with untreated depression, the risk of suicidal thoughts and attempts is meaningfully higher than in the general population. Not everyone experiences this, but it is the cost no other line item compares with, and the reason real treatment matters. If you or someone you love is in danger now, call 988, the Suicide and Crisis Lifeline.

What treatment looks like

Three levels of depression treatment we provide

Mental Wellness KS offers three levels of depression treatment for adults at our Palm Springs program: residential, partial hospitalization (PHP), and intensive outpatient (IOP). The right level depends on the severity of symptoms, the support already in place, and what each day looks like. Our clinical team recommends the level that fits, and clients can move between levels of care as their needs change.

Residential depression treatment

24-hour care · 30–45 days · up to 12 adults in residence

Residential depression treatment is typically the right level when symptoms make daily functioning difficult, when 24-hour clinical contact is needed, or when a medication regimen needs daily psychiatric supervision while it stabilizes. Clients sleep at the house, a private single-family home in Palm Springs, not a hospital. A psychiatric evaluation with Dr. Christian Small, MD happens in the first 24 hours; medications are reviewed and, where appropriate, adjusted.

The clinical week includes individual therapy with a primary therapist, group programming five days a week, family sessions when the client is ready, and daily contact with the psychiatric team. The modalities are evidence-based: CBT, behavioral activation, DBT skills, EMDR, and Cognitive Processing Therapy for trauma-rooted presentations. The medication work, the therapy, and the structure all run in the same building, by the same small team.

Partial Hospitalization Program (PHP) for depression

Mon–Fri · 8 AM – 3 PM · 6–12 weeks, typical

A partial hospitalization program for depression is often the right level for clients who can sleep safely at home or in supportive housing but still need full clinical days. Six hours of structured programming, five days a week, with evenings and weekends spent outside the clinical setting.

The clinical content holds the same intensity as residential: continued psychotherapy, ongoing psychiatric oversight, group, and family work. The day is structured, and the home routine resumes around it. Some clients start at PHP; others arrive here after residential.

Intensive Outpatient Program (IOP) for depression

Mon–Fri · morning or afternoon track · 6–12 weeks, typical

An intensive outpatient program for depression is often the right level when clients are re-engaging with work, school, or family responsibilities and need to keep the clinical rhythm without losing the day. Three hours a day, five days a week, on a morning (8–11) or afternoon (12–3) schedule.

Whether a client starts at IOP or arrives here from a higher level of care, the same primary therapist, psychiatric provider, and peer group stay in place. Continuity of relationship is one of the most under-discussed predictors of depression recovery, and we protect it deliberately.

Talk about it

Healthy ways to talk about depression

The right phrase at the right moment can change the trajectory. Below are phrases that tend to land well, and what to do if the conversation turns toward safety.

Try saying

  • “I have noticed you haven't been yourself lately. I want to understand.”
  • “You don't have to explain. I just want you to know I see it.”
  • “What is one small thing I can do this week that would actually help?”
  • “Would it help if I came with you to that appointment?”
  • “I'm not going anywhere. We figure this out together.”

If your loved one mentions thoughts of self-harm or suicide, take it seriously. Ask directly: “Are you thinking about hurting yourself?” The question does not put the thought there. It does open the door. Then call 988, the national Suicide and Crisis Lifeline, or go to your nearest emergency room.

Common questions

Frequently asked questions about depression treatment

How common is depression in adults?

Approximately 21 million adults in the United States experience at least one major depressive episode each year. It is among the most common mental-health conditions in adulthood and, when treated correctly, among the most responsive. The challenge is rarely whether treatment works, it is whether the level of care matches the severity.

What is treatment-resistant depression and how is it different?

Treatment-resistant depression (TRD) is clinically defined as depression that has not responded adequately to at least two appropriate antidepressant trials at therapeutic dose and duration. In practice, the clients we admit under this label have often been through three or more medications. TRD is rarely a sign that recovery is impossible, it usually means the next step needs more structure than a monthly psychiatry appointment can provide.

How do I know if my depression needs residential treatment?

Residential is the right level when 24-hour clinical contact is needed: active suicidal thinking, an inability to perform basic self-care, recent psychiatric hospitalization, or a medication regimen that needs daily supervision while it stabilizes. PHP is for clients who can sleep at home or in supportive housing but still need full clinical days. IOP is the step-down. Admissions will walk you through which level fits your situation.

What if my loved one refuses treatment?

This is one of the most common questions we hear. Most adults with depression do not reach out for treatment on their own. What works is patience, specific concern (not general worry), and making the first step very small, “I want to make one phone call together this week” rather than “you need to go to treatment.” Call admissions and we can coach you on the conversation before you have it.

How long does depression treatment take?

A typical course at Mental Wellness KS is 30 to 45 days residential, followed by 6 to 12 weeks of PHP and IOP. Most clients notice the first changes in sleep, appetite, and energy within two to three weeks. Mood and motivation usually begin shifting in weeks four to six.

What if I am in crisis right now?

If you are in immediate danger, call or text 988, the national Suicide and Crisis Lifeline, or go to your nearest emergency room. Mental Wellness KS is not a 24-hour crisis line. Call (866) 460-4462 when you are ready to talk about the work that comes next.

Do you offer TMS, ketamine, or ECT?

No. Mental Wellness KS does not provide TMS, ketamine-assisted therapy, or ECT on-site. We treat depression, including treatment-resistant depression, with intensive psychotherapy, behavioral activation, psychiatric medication management under a board-certified psychiatrist, and the structure of a residential or outpatient program. If TMS or ECT becomes clinically indicated, we coordinate referrals with trusted local providers.
