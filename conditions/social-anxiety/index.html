About social anxiety

What is social anxiety?

Social anxiety disorder is a persistent fear of being negatively judged in social or performance situations. The fear is severe enough that it causes real distress and pushes the person to avoid situations they actually want to be in. It is not the same as shyness. Shyness is a temperament; social anxiety is a disorder. The line is drawn at how much the fear is narrowing the life.

Roughly 7% of U.S. adults meet criteria for social anxiety in any given year. Onset is usually in adolescence or early adulthood. Untreated, social anxiety tends to compound, because every avoided situation tells the brain that the situation was indeed too dangerous to approach. That is the loop the treatment is designed to interrupt.

Two forms

Performance anxiety and interaction anxiety

Social anxiety shows up in two recognizable forms. Most people have some of both, but one is usually dominant, and it matters for the treatment plan. Performance anxiety responds well to short-term tools, including beta-blockers used as needed. Interaction anxiety needs the longer therapy work.

Performance anxiety

A specific kind of situation, often with a clear audience

Public speaking, presentations, performing in front of a class, sitting an exam, sometimes eating or writing in front of others. The body's stress response (racing heart, shaking, sweating) becomes the source of the fear itself, because the person worries the audience will see it. Beta-blockers are sometimes used for specific situations, and CBT helps with the underlying interpretation.

Interaction anxiety

The broader, more pervasive form

Small talk, meeting new people, dating, joining a conversation in progress, ordering at a counter, being on a phone call, eating in a cafeteria. The fear is not about a single performance; it is about being negatively evaluated in any unscripted social moment. This form is what most people mean when they say social anxiety, and it is what residential is most useful for.

Symptoms

Signs and symptoms of social anxiety disorder

Social anxiety symptoms group into four categories. Most people with the disorder have some symptoms in each, but the dominant category often shapes the treatment plan. Both physical-symptom-driven and cognitive-rumination-driven presentations respond to the same evidence-based treatments, just at different paces.

Emotional symptoms

  • Intense fear of being judged, criticized, or rejected
  • Persistent worry about embarrassing yourself in front of others
  • Dread building up for hours or days before a social event
  • Shame or self-criticism after social interactions
  • Feeling self-conscious or "watched" in everyday settings

Physical symptoms

  • Blushing, sweating, or hot flashes
  • Rapid heartbeat or pounding in the chest
  • Trembling hands, shaking voice
  • Nausea or stomach upset before social situations
  • Dizziness, dry mouth, muscle tension

Behavioral symptoms

  • Avoiding social or performance situations entirely
  • Skipping work, school, or events you actually wanted to attend
  • Using alcohol or other substances to manage anxiety
  • Speaking softly, dropping eye contact, leaving early
  • Heavy rehearsal or scripting of routine conversations

Cognitive symptoms

  • Replaying conversations for hours or days afterward
  • Catastrophic predictions: "I will freeze," "they will see I am nervous"
  • Mind going blank in social situations
  • Excessive self-monitoring during interactions
  • Persistent belief that others see flaws you cannot disguise

Causes

What causes social anxiety?

Social anxiety disorder rarely has a single cause. Most cases come from a combination of biological vulnerability, learned patterns, and life events that reinforced the fear. Understanding which factors are most prominent in your picture helps shape the treatment plan.

Biological factors

Social anxiety runs in families. Studies of twins suggest a meaningful genetic component. Differences in the amygdala, the brain's threat-detection center, and in serotonin signaling appear in research on social anxiety. None of this means the condition is fixed; biology is the starting point, not the ending one.

Environmental factors

Childhood experiences of bullying, social rejection, public embarrassment, harsh criticism, or family environments where mistakes were punished tend to set the conditions for social anxiety to develop. Overprotective parenting that prevented age-appropriate social practice can also play a role.

Psychological factors

A temperament marked by behavioral inhibition (cautiousness in new situations as a young child) is one of the strongest predictors of later social anxiety. So is a tendency toward perfectionism, high self-criticism, and the belief that any social misstep will produce lasting judgment.

Risk factors that compound

Co-occurring depression, anxiety, or substance use raises social-anxiety severity over time. So does prolonged avoidance, which gradually narrows the range of situations a person can re-enter without help. Earlier age of onset and longer time untreated are both associated with more severe presentations.

The impact of social anxiety

How social anxiety affects daily life

Untreated social anxiety does not stay quiet. Over time, it begins to shape decisions a person did not even realize were being shaped. Recognizing where the disorder is showing up in your life is often the first step toward deciding the cost of leaving it untreated is higher than the cost of getting help.

Work and career

Adults with social anxiety often turn down promotions that require more visibility, avoid networking, sit silent in meetings even when they know the answer, and leave roles when public-facing responsibilities increase. The career cost is often the slowest to be noticed and the largest in total.

Relationships and dating

Social anxiety makes it harder to start new friendships, harder to date, and harder to maintain the small ongoing social effort that long-term relationships require. Many adults with the disorder describe years of loneliness inside a life that, from the outside, looks fine.

Education

Students with social anxiety may avoid classes that require presentations, drop majors with more group work, or leave school entirely when the social load exceeds what they can sustain. The academic trajectory often does not match the underlying ability.

Mental and physical health

Untreated social anxiety raises the risk of depression, alcohol use disorder, and other anxiety conditions. The chronic stress response also takes a measurable physical toll over time: sleep disruption, gastrointestinal issues, and elevated cardiovascular risk all appear at higher rates.

Our treatment

How we treat social anxiety

Cognitive behavioral therapy with graded exposure is first-line and has the strongest evidence base for social anxiety. The work has two parts: changing what the brain is predicting, and giving the brain new data by actually entering the feared situation while the prediction is being tested. Residential makes the second part much easier, because the situations are built into every day.

CBT with cognitive restructuring
Identifying the specific catastrophic predictions, slowing them down, and learning to design experiments that test them. This is not positive thinking. It is the patient work of figuring out what the brain is actually predicting and then checking the prediction against what happens.
Graded in-vivo exposure
An exposure ladder built together with your therapist. Each rung is a real situation, just slightly more uncomfortable than the last. You stay in the situation long enough for the anxiety to crest and start to come down on its own. The point is the new data, not the discomfort itself.
Group therapy as built-in exposure
Group is one of the most useful pieces of the program. A room of other people doing the same work, talking about the same predictions, is itself an exposure environment. Many clients find their first real conversations about social anxiety happen in group.
Psychiatric care for medication
Dr. Christian Small, MD, our Medical Director, evaluates and adjusts medication when it is part of the plan. SSRIs and SNRIs are the workhorses. Beta-blockers are used situationally for performance anxiety where physical symptoms are the main barrier.

Levels of care

Residential, PHP, and IOP for social anxiety

Most adults with social anxiety can get better with weekly outpatient CBT, and that is the right first step for many. The levels of care below are designed for people whose avoidance has narrowed life enough that weekly outpatient cannot get them to the appointment, or whose social anxiety is layered with depression, substance use, or another condition that needs the structure of a real program.

Residential

30 to 45 days

Residential social anxiety treatment

24-hour care in a small program. Daily CBT, individual therapy several times a week, graded exposure work built into ordinary residential life (meals together, group discussions, brief outings), and psychiatric care for medication. The right level when prior outpatient has not held, when depression or substance use is layered on top, or when the avoidance has reached the point that leaving the house is itself a barrier.

PHP

6 to 12 weeks

Partial Hospitalization Program

Six hours of structured programming a day, weekdays, evenings spent at home or in supported housing. Group CBT, individual therapy, and continued exposure work. The natural step down from residential, and a strong standalone option for clients with severe social anxiety who do not need 24-hour care but do need more than weekly therapy.

IOP

6 to 12 weeks

Intensive Outpatient Program

Three hours of programming, three days a week. Designed to coexist with returning to work, school, or family. The exposure ladder you started in higher levels of care continues, but now most of the rungs are happening in your real life. Most clients step down to community-based outpatient therapy after IOP and use what they have built here as the foundation.

Medication

Medication for social anxiety

Medication is not required to treat social anxiety, but it helps for many people, particularly when the anxiety is severe or when depression is layered on top. The treatment guidelines are clear about which medications work.

SSRIs (sertraline, paroxetine, fluvoxamine, escitalopram) and the SNRI venlafaxine are the workhorses. They reduce the general level of anxiety enough that the exposure work has room to happen. They take six to twelve weeks at a stable dose to show their full effect, which is why starting them in residential is often a clean approach: the rest of the work proceeds while the medication ramps up.

Beta-blockers like propranolol are different. They are used situationally, before a specific performance event, to blunt the physical symptoms (racing heart, shaky hands, sweating). They do nothing for the underlying belief, and they are not a long-term solution, but they can be a useful tool while the rest of the work is happening.

Benzodiazepines (alprazolam, lorazepam) are used carefully. They reduce anxiety fast, and they also blunt the exposure learning that is doing the long-term healing. Dr. Christian Small, MD, will not prescribe them lightly for social anxiety.

Insurance coverage

If the call itself is the hard part, here's what to expect

Most people with social anxiety dread the inquiry call more than any other part of the process. We know this, and the call is built around that fact. A clinician answers, not a call center. Here is what we ask, and what we don't.

What we'll ask

  • Your name and the best way to reach you back
  • A short version of what is going on, in whatever words you have for it
  • Your insurance carrier and member ID, so we can verify benefits while you are on the line
  • What level of care you are looking at, residential or outpatient, or what questions you have about the difference
  • Whether anyone else in your life knows you are calling, and whether you want them involved

What we won't ask

  • For specifics you are not ready to share. You can stay vague on a first call
  • For a credit card. Verification of benefits is free, and there is no charge for the conversation
  • For a commitment. Calling is not admitting. People call to learn what their options are; many do not call back, and that is fine
  • For the names or contact information of anyone in your life who does not need to be on the form
  • For you to make a decision on the phone. The phone call is information, not a contract

Common questions

Frequently asked questions about social anxiety treatment

What is the difference between shyness and social anxiety?

Shyness is a temperament trait. It is uncomfortable but it does not usually narrow a life. Social anxiety is a clinical disorder marked by a degree of fear that drives the person to avoid situations they actually want to be in: work meetings, dates, classes, friendships. The clinical line is drawn at distress and impairment, not at the presence of nervousness itself.

Can I do residential treatment if making the call is the hard part?

Yes, and this is exactly the population the program was designed for. A clinician will answer, not a call center. You can ask whatever you want. You can also send a written inquiry through our contact form if a phone call is too much at the start. The first conversation is shorter than people expect, and there is no commitment in making it.

Will I have to do exposure work I'm not ready for?

No. Exposure work in CBT for social anxiety is graded, meaning it climbs one step at a time. You and your therapist build the ladder together. Nothing happens on it that you have not agreed to. The point of the work is not pushing through; it is staying long enough in a situation that the prediction your brain made about it turns out to be wrong.

Are there medications for social anxiety?

Yes. SSRIs and SNRIs are first-line and have strong evidence for generalized social anxiety. Sertraline, paroxetine, fluvoxamine, escitalopram, and venlafaxine all appear in the guidelines. Beta-blockers like propranolol are used situationally for performance anxiety where the physical symptoms (racing heart, shaky hands) are the main problem. Benzodiazepines are used cautiously because they can blunt the exposure work that is doing the long-term healing.

Why residential instead of weekly outpatient therapy?

Most people with social anxiety can do well in outpatient CBT, and that is the right first step for many. Residential is the right level of care when the avoidance has already narrowed the life enough that the person cannot get to outpatient consistently, when depression or substance use is layered on top, or when prior outpatient attempts have not held. The structure of residential is also a built-in graded-exposure environment that outpatient cannot replicate.

Will my insurance cover social anxiety treatment?

Most commercial plans cover residential, PHP, and IOP for social anxiety disorder when medically necessary. We are in-network with most major carriers and also accept out-of-network. The 10-minute verification call tells you exactly what your specific plan covers before you commit to anything. Call (866) 460-4462.

Will I have to do group therapy?

Group is part of the program and one of the most useful pieces, but how much you say and how soon is set by you and your therapist. Many clients with social anxiety find that being in a room with other people doing the same work is itself the first piece of exposure. Listening counts. Talking comes when it comes.
