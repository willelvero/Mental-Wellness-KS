About psychosis

Psychosis is a symptom, not a diagnosis

This is the most important sentence on the page. Psychosis is a state, not an illness. It refers to a break with shared reality, usually some combination of hallucinations, delusions, disorganized thinking, and a general sense to the people around the person that what is being said no longer tracks. That state can occur as part of many different conditions, each of which has its own treatment path.

Roughly 3% of people will experience a psychotic episode at some point in their lives. First episodes most commonly happen between ages 16 and 30. The single best predictor of long-term outcome is how soon real treatment begins after symptoms appear. Within the first year is the window that matters most.

What it looks like

What a psychotic episode looks like

Psychosis is usually described in two categories. The positive symptoms are the experiences added on top of normal functioning. The negative symptoms are the things that get quieter or disappear. Most episodes include some of both. A good clinical picture pays attention to both.

Positive symptoms

Experiences added that were not there before

  • Hallucinations. Most often hearing voices that other people do not hear. Less commonly visual, tactile, or olfactory. The voices may comment, command, or argue.
  • Delusions. Fixed false beliefs that hold up against contradicting evidence. Paranoid (being watched, followed, poisoned), grandiose (having special powers, mission, identity), or referential (events on the news are messages meant for the person).
  • Disorganized thinking. Speech that jumps tracks, loses the thread, or connects ideas in ways the listener cannot follow.
  • Disorganized behavior. Unusual movements, dressing in ways that do not fit the weather, agitation without obvious cause.

Negative symptoms

Capacities that get quieter or disappear

  • Flat affect. Reduced facial expression and vocal modulation. The face stops moving the way it used to.
  • Avolition. Loss of motivation to start or complete normal activities. Days spent in bed without an obvious depression.
  • Alogia. Reduced speech output. Short answers, long silences, even when there is no fear in the room.
  • Social withdrawal. Pulling away from friends, family, work, school. Often the first thing the people around the person notice.
  • Anhedonia. Reduced ability to feel pleasure in things that used to bring it.

Causes

What can cause psychosis

Because psychosis is a symptom rather than a diagnosis, the same break-with-reality picture can come from very different underlying causes. The clinical work begins with figuring out which one. The treatment plan looks different for each.

Schizophrenia spectrum

Schizophrenia, schizoaffective disorder, and schizophreniform disorder are the most familiar source of psychosis. Onset is usually late teens to mid-twenties. Treatment is long-term and built around antipsychotic medication. See our schizophrenia page for the dedicated picture.

Bipolar disorder with psychotic features

Severe manic and depressive episodes can include psychotic symptoms. The content of the psychosis often mirrors the mood state: grandiose delusions in mania, nihilistic ones in deep depression. Treating the underlying bipolar disorder typically resolves the psychosis.

Severe depression with psychotic features

Major depressive episodes can include psychotic symptoms, usually mood-congruent: delusions of guilt, deservedness of punishment, ruin, or that one is already dead. Carries a higher suicide risk than non-psychotic depression and is treated with antidepressant plus antipsychotic, sometimes ECT.

Substance-induced psychosis

Methamphetamine, cocaine, cannabis (particularly high-potency THC), psychedelics, and PCP can all trigger psychotic episodes. Alcohol withdrawal can produce hallucinations. Some episodes resolve when the substance clears; others persist and signal an underlying vulnerability that is going to need ongoing treatment.

Postpartum psychosis

A rare but serious form of psychosis that emerges in the days or first few weeks after childbirth. Carries a real risk of harm to both mother and infant and is a psychiatric emergency. Treatment is hospital-based first, then a careful step-down to residential or outpatient care.

Medical and neurological causes

Brain tumors, dementias, autoimmune encephalitis, certain seizure disorders, severe infections, and some medications can cause psychotic symptoms. Medical workup is part of the differential when psychosis is new and the picture is atypical for a primary psychiatric cause.

Early signs

Early warning signs before a first episode

Most first episodes are preceded by a prodromal phase, a stretch of weeks or months in which something is clearly off but the picture has not yet become frankly psychotic. Catching the prodrome and getting evaluation early is one of the highest-leverage moves in the whole field. Families are often the first to see these changes.

  1. 01

    Withdrawal from friends and activities

    A noticeable pulling away from social connections, hobbies, and routines the person used to value. Often the first change families register, sometimes mistaken for ordinary depression or a phase.

  2. 02

    Sleep and routine changes

    Reversed day-night cycle, long stretches of insomnia, or sleeping at unusual hours. Personal routines (showering, eating regular meals, going to class) begin to drift.

  3. 03

    A new suspiciousness

    The person starts reading meaning into ordinary events. Friends are talking behind their back, the news is referring to them, the way someone glanced is significant. The suspicion may still respond to reassurance early on; later it does not.

  4. 04

    Unusual sensory experiences

    Brief, attenuated versions of what later become full hallucinations: hearing one's own name when no one called it, a sense of a presence in the room, lights or shadows that feel meaningful. The person may or may not mention these.

  5. 05

    A general sense that something is off

    Family members often say in retrospect that they knew for months. The conversations did not quite track. The eye contact felt different. There was no single symptom they could name, just a quiet certainty that something had changed. Trust that.

Levels of care

Where to start: hospital first, then us

Active acute psychosis with safety concerns is a hospital-level event. That is not a comment on the person; it is a comment on what the environment needs to provide. Mental Wellness KS is the step that comes after. We do not duplicate hospital crisis stabilization, and we are clear about why.

Phase 1

Acute psychiatric hospitalization

For an active episode with safety concerns or rapidly worsening symptoms. A locked psychiatric unit with 24-hour staffing, emergency medical resources, and the ability to start antipsychotic medication quickly. Typical stay: 5 to 10 days. Goal: stabilization, not full recovery.

Phase 2

Residential at Mental Wellness KS

Once stabilized, the work shifts to getting medication tuned, rebuilding routines, doing the therapy that the acute phase made impossible, and bringing the family in. Typical stay: 30 to 45 days. Discharge is not the end of treatment; it is the start of the next phase.

Phase 3

PHP, then IOP, then community

A graduated step-down. Partial Hospitalization Program (6-hour days, weekdays) followed by Intensive Outpatient Program (3 hours, three days a week), followed by community-based outpatient psychiatry and therapy. Each step preserves more of the client's daily life while still holding the structure that prevents relapse.

If your loved one is currently in an acute episode and not yet hospitalized, call 988 for the Suicide and Crisis Lifeline (they handle psychotic crises too) or 911 if there is an immediate safety concern. Our admissions team can also help you understand the sequence on the phone, even before the hospital step is in motion.

Our treatment

How we treat psychosis after stabilization

The residential phase is when the foundation gets built. Medication is tuned in days rather than the months it takes outpatient. Routines that psychosis erodes get rebuilt with structure around them. The family learns the illness alongside the client. The therapy work begins as soon as the person is well enough to engage with it.

Antipsychotic medication management
Dr. Christian Small, MD, our Medical Director, evaluates the regimen the client arrives on, adjusts where adjustments are clinically warranted, and monitors side effects daily. Most clients leave on a regimen that is meaningfully better tuned than the one they came in with.
Cognitive Behavioral Therapy for psychosis (CBTp)
An evidence-based therapy specifically developed for psychotic symptoms. CBTp helps clients identify and work with voices and delusions in ways that reduce their distress and the behaviors that follow from them. It does not require disagreeing with the experiences; it works within them.
Routine, sleep, and basic structure
Psychosis erodes routine, and routine in turn is one of the strongest stabilizing forces against relapse. The daily schedule is part of the medicine: regular meals, consistent sleep, predictable group sessions, time outside.
Family education and relapse-prevention planning
Family education has one of the strongest evidence bases in psychosis treatment. We build a written relapse-prevention plan with the client and family together: the early warning signs to watch for, who to call first, and what specific steps come next.

The impact of psychosis

How a psychotic episode affects daily life

A psychotic episode is not a discrete event that ends and is over. The acute symptoms can resolve in days or weeks with treatment, but the practical aftermath, in relationships, in routines, and in confidence, often takes much longer. Recognizing the full footprint is part of the planning for what comes next.

Trust in your own perceptions

After a psychotic episode, many people find it hard to trust their own thoughts and senses for some time. This is normal, and it eases as stability returns. Working through it is part of the therapy after stabilization.

Family and close relationships

An episode is hard on the people around the person. Family members often need their own support, and the relationship needs intentional repair. Both happen in family work during residential and PHP.

Work, school, and identity

A psychotic episode can interrupt education, employment, and the sense of self that runs through them. Returning to those takes patience and pacing. The goal is not back to where you were the day before; it is forward into a stable place.

Long-term outlook

For many people, particularly after a first episode, full recovery is realistic. For others, the picture is one of long-term management of an underlying condition. Either way, the trajectory is much better when treatment is consistent and continued past the acute phase. If someone is in crisis now, call 988 or 911.

Medication

Antipsychotic medication overview

Antipsychotic medication is the foundation of treatment when psychosis is part of the picture. Getting the regimen right often takes more than one try. A residential stay makes that easier because adjustments can be made and observed in days rather than the months that outpatient follow-ups require.

Second-generation (atypical) antipsychotics

Risperidone, olanzapine, aripiprazole, paliperidone, quetiapine, lurasidone, ziprasidone. The first-line class for most adults. Side-effect profiles vary: olanzapine carries more metabolic effects, aripiprazole tends to have less sedation, risperidone has clean efficacy data. The choice is individual.

First-generation (typical) antipsychotics

Haloperidol, fluphenazine, chlorpromazine. Older medications, still effective. More movement-related side effects than the newer class, but for some clients the symptom control is genuinely better. Sometimes used in long-acting injectable form.

Long-acting injectables

Risperidone, paliperidone, aripiprazole, olanzapine, haloperidol, and fluphenazine are all available as injections given every two to four weeks. Often a good fit after a first episode for clients who want the medication decision off their daily plate. They are also associated with lower relapse rates than oral antipsychotics for many people.

Clozapine

The most effective antipsychotic, reserved for clients who have not responded to two adequate trials of other medications. Requires regular blood-test monitoring. For the right client, it is sometimes life-changing.

This is an overview, not prescribing advice. Every regimen at Mental Wellness KS is set and adjusted by Dr. Christian Small, MD, our board-certified Medical Director, in close coordination with the rest of the clinical team.

Common questions

Frequently asked questions about psychosis treatment

Is psychosis the same thing as schizophrenia?

No. Psychosis is a symptom; schizophrenia is one of several diagnoses that include psychosis. A person can experience a psychotic episode as part of severe depression, bipolar disorder, postpartum hormonal change, substance use, sleep deprivation, or medical and neurological conditions. The right treatment depends on what is causing the psychosis. Schizophrenia is a chronic condition defined by persistent psychotic symptoms plus negative and cognitive symptoms for six months or longer. See our dedicated schizophrenia page for that condition.

What should I do if a loved one is in active psychosis right now?

Acute, unmanaged psychosis with safety concerns is treated at hospital level first. Call 988 for the Suicide and Crisis Lifeline (they handle psychotic crises too), 911 if there is an immediate danger, or take the person to a psychiatric emergency room. Residential treatment is the step that comes after the acute phase has been stabilized. Our admissions team can help you understand this sequence on the phone, even if your loved one is currently in a hospital.

When is residential treatment the right level of care for psychosis?

Residential at Mental Wellness KS is the right level after a person has been stabilized to the point that they can engage with daily programming. That usually means recently discharged from a hospital, currently on antipsychotic medication that is starting to work, and ready to do the recovery work in a structured environment. We do not provide acute crisis stabilization; we are the step that follows.

What are the early warning signs before a first episode?

Most first episodes are preceded by a prodromal phase that can last weeks or months. The signs include withdrawal from friends and activities, changes in sleep, declining work or school performance, unusual sensory experiences or magical thinking, suspiciousness that is new for the person, and a general sense from family that something is off in a way they cannot quite name. Catching the prodrome and getting evaluation early significantly improves long-term outcomes.

What medications are used to treat psychosis?

Antipsychotic medications are the foundation. Second-generation (atypical) antipsychotics like risperidone, olanzapine, aripiprazole, paliperidone, and quetiapine are first-line for most adults. First-generation antipsychotics like haloperidol are still used in some cases. Long-acting injectable forms are available for adults who want them. For psychosis driven by another condition (severe depression, bipolar disorder), the underlying condition is treated simultaneously. All medication at Mental Wellness KS is set and adjusted by Dr. Christian Small, MD.

Can someone fully recover from a psychotic episode?

Many people do, particularly when the episode is the first one, when treatment begins quickly, and when the underlying cause is something treatable like substance use or severe depression. For psychosis that is part of schizophrenia or schizoaffective disorder, the goal is sustained stability rather than cure, and that is achievable for the majority of clients with consistent treatment. Outcomes depend heavily on the cause and on how soon care begins.
