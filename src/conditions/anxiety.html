What it is

What is an anxiety disorder?

An anxiety disorder is fear or worry that does not correspond to what is actually in front of you, persists for weeks or months, and starts to interfere with sleep, work, relationships, or your sense of safety.

Anxiety disorders are among the most common mental-health conditions in adults in the United States, and among the most treatable. The challenge is rarely whether treatment works. It is whether the level of care matches the severity, and whether the people delivering it actually know you.

At Mental Wellness KS, we treat anxiety in adults, including active-duty service members and veterans, at three levels of care, in a small residential setting in Palm Springs designed to support the actual work of getting well.

Conditions we work with

Types of anxiety we treat

Anxiety is an umbrella term. Underneath it are several distinct conditions with different presentations, different triggers, and different evidence-based treatments. These are the ones we see most often in adults.

Generalized Anxiety Disorder (GAD)

Chronic, low-grade worry that runs in the background of daily life and refuses to switch off, even when there is nothing in particular to worry about. Often accompanied by muscle tension, sleep disturbance, irritability, and trouble concentrating. GAD is one of the most common adult anxiety disorders we treat.

Panic Disorder

Sudden, intense surges of fear, chest tightness, racing heart, the conviction that something catastrophic is about to happen, that come on without warning. Many clients with panic disorder also develop a secondary fear of the attacks themselves, which narrows the world until normal life becomes hard to navigate.

Social Anxiety Disorder

A persistent, intense fear of being observed, judged, or humiliated in social or performance situations. Distinct from ordinary shyness, social anxiety can keep adults from work, school, dating, and family events. It responds well to a combination of cognitive-behavioral therapy and gradual exposure.

Specific Phobias

An out-of-proportion fear of a particular object or situation, flying, heights, blood, enclosed spaces, certain animals, that prompts avoidance. Phobias are highly treatable; most respond to short-term exposure-based therapy when delivered well.

Agoraphobia

Fear of being in places or situations from which escape would be difficult, often co-occurring with panic disorder. Over time, agoraphobia can shrink the world to a single room. We treat it with structured, supported exposure and the slow rebuilding of safe contact with the outside.

Trauma-Related Anxiety & PTSD

Anxiety that traces back to a specific traumatic event, combat, assault, accident, sustained childhood adversity, and presents as hyperarousal, intrusive memories, nightmares, and avoidance. We treat trauma-related anxiety using EMDR and Cognitive Processing Therapy (CPT), and we run a dedicated track for veterans.

A note on OCD and acute psychosis. Obsessive-compulsive disorder is no longer classified as an anxiety disorder in modern diagnostic criteria, though it often presents alongside one. We treat OCD as part of our broader mental-health programming. We do not admit clients in acute, active psychosis; admissions screens carefully on a case-by-case basis.

Symptoms

Signs and symptoms of an anxiety disorder

Anxiety shows up in four overlapping ways. Most adults with an anxiety disorder have symptoms in every category; the dominant one usually shapes which therapy and which medication will help most.

01

Emotional

Persistent dread, irritability, a sense that something bad is about to happen, and inability to feel calm even when nothing in the room is wrong. A nervous system that will not stand down.

02

Physical

Racing or pounding heart, tight chest, shortness of breath, muscle tension and headaches, gastrointestinal upset, sweating, restlessness, and broken sleep. The body acting like it is in danger when it is not.

03

Cognitive

Worry that loops and will not switch off, catastrophic predictions about ordinary situations, difficulty concentrating, mind going blank, intrusive what-ifs, and constant scanning for threat.

04

Behavioral

Avoidance of triggering situations, reassurance-seeking from family or partners, checking and re-checking, alcohol or substance use to take the edge off, and gradually narrowing the range of things daily life can include.

Causes

What causes anxiety disorders?

Most cases come from a combination of factors that interact over time. Identifying which ones are doing the most work in your picture is part of the early treatment assessment.

Biological vulnerability
Anxiety disorders run in families. Twin studies suggest a meaningful genetic contribution. Differences in serotonin, GABA, and noradrenergic systems all show up in the anxiety research. Biology sets the floor, not the ceiling.
Trauma and stressful life events
Accidents, assaults, combat exposure, childhood adversity, prolonged caregiving, illness, and grief can all trigger anxiety disorders in people who never had clinical anxiety before. The trauma does not have to be a single dramatic event to count.
Medical contributors
Thyroid disorders, cardiac conditions, certain stimulants (caffeine, prescription stimulants), some asthma medications, and chronic alcohol or substance use all produce or worsen anxiety symptoms. A medical workup is part of any thorough evaluation.
Risk factors that compound
Co-occurring depression, sleep deprivation, social isolation, and untreated trauma are all factors that turn manageable anxiety into a disorder. So is the avoidance that anxiety produces, which gradually shrinks the range of situations a person can re-enter.

How we treat it

The therapies we use

Anxiety responds to specific therapies delivered well, not to a buffet of options. These are the modalities our clinical team uses most often.

  • Cognitive Behavioral Therapy (CBT)

    The first-line, evidence-based therapy for most anxiety disorders. Identifies the thought patterns that drive the anxiety response and replaces them with more accurate, more useful ones.

  • Dialectical Behavior Therapy (DBT)

    Skills-based work, distress tolerance, emotion regulation, interpersonal effectiveness, that gives clients concrete tools for the moments when anxiety spikes.

  • Exposure-Based Therapy

    Structured, supported exposure to the situations or thoughts that drive avoidance. The most powerful tool for panic disorder, specific phobias, social anxiety, and agoraphobia.

  • EMDR (Eye Movement Desensitization & Reprocessing)

    An evidence-based protocol for anxiety rooted in trauma. We use EMDR when a discrete traumatic memory is driving the symptom picture.

  • Cognitive Processing Therapy (CPT)

    A 12-session, structured therapy originally developed for PTSD in veterans. We use it heavily in our veteran mental-health track and with clients whose anxiety carries a trauma component.

  • Psychodynamic & Family Therapy

    Insight-oriented individual work, plus family sessions where the home environment is part of the picture. Family therapy is included with every level of care.

  • Psychiatric Care & Medication Management

    Board-certified psychiatric oversight from Dr. Christian Small, MD. We do not over-prescribe and we do not change long-standing regimens abruptly. Medication is one tool among many.

  • Mindfulness, Fitness, & the Day-to-Day

    Mindfulness practice, group fitness, art therapy, outings, and shared meals. The structure of an ordinary, well-lived day is part of how anxiety quiets down.

Common questions

Frequently asked questions about anxiety treatment

What is the difference between anxiety and an anxiety disorder?

Anxiety is a normal human response to stress or uncertainty. An anxiety disorder is when that response stops corresponding to what's actually in front of you, persists for weeks or months, and starts interfering with sleep, work, relationships, or your sense of safety. Anxiety disorders are common, and they respond well to treatment.

How do I know if I need residential, PHP, or IOP?

Residential is for adults whose symptoms have outpaced what outpatient therapy can hold, frequent panic attacks, severe avoidance, suicidal thinking, or co-occurring conditions that need 24-hour care. PHP is a structured weekday program for clients who are stable enough to sleep at home or in our supportive housing but still need clinical contact most days. IOP is a step-down, three hours a day, three to five days a week, for clients re-entering work, school, or family life. Admissions will walk you through which fits.

How long does anxiety treatment take at Mental Wellness KS?

A typical residential stay is 30 to 45 days, followed by 6 to 12 weeks of PHP and IOP. Anxiety treatment is rarely linear, clients often step up and down between levels as symptoms shift. The clinical team reassesses fit every week.

Do you treat veterans with anxiety or PTSD?

Yes. Mental Wellness KS is veteran-owned, both founders are certified trauma professionals, and we run a dedicated veteran mental-health track. We are in-network with TRICARE and TriWest, and we routinely treat active-duty service members and veterans whose anxiety overlaps with combat-related PTSD or moral injury.

Can I continue the psychiatric medication I'm already taking?

In nearly all cases, yes. Our Medical Director, Dr. Christian Small, MD, is a board-certified psychiatrist who reviews every client's regimen on admission. If a change is clinically indicated, it is made carefully and collaboratively, not abruptly.

How is treatment here different from a psychiatric hospital?

Mental Wellness KS is not a hospital. It is a small residential program in a single-family home in Palm Springs, no fluorescent corridors, no rotating staff, no locked doors. We are licensed and clinically rigorous, but the environment is built to look and feel like a place you can actually live in while doing the work of getting well.
