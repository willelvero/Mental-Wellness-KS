Specialized care

OCD treatment starts with specialized ERP care

OCD treatment is most effective when it directly targets the cycle of obsessions, anxiety, compulsions, and temporary relief. For many adults, general talk therapy is not enough, because OCD requires a specialized approach that helps clients face triggers without performing rituals or reassurance-seeking behaviors.

Exposure and Response Prevention, also called ERP, is the primary therapy used to treat OCD. ERP helps clients gradually approach feared thoughts, images, sensations, or situations while learning not to complete the compulsion that keeps the OCD cycle alive. At Mental Wellness KS, ERP is supported by psychiatric care, structured therapy, family education, and multiple levels of care, including residential treatment, PHP, and IOP.

The primary therapy for OCD

What ERP therapy for OCD looks like

Exposure and Response Prevention helps clients practice facing OCD triggers without completing the compulsion that normally follows. Over time, the brain learns that anxiety can rise and fall without rituals, checking, reassurance, avoidance, or mental neutralizing.

ERP is collaborative and planned. The client and therapist build an exposure hierarchy, sometimes called a ladder, starting with lower-intensity triggers and gradually working toward more difficult ones. Exposures are not designed to overwhelm or shame the client. They are structured, agreed upon, and guided by the treatment team.

An example exposure ladder

Lower rungs first · built with your therapist

  1. 5 Stay in the feared situation long enough for anxiety to decrease without rituals
  2. 4 Face the trigger without performing the compulsion
  3. 3 Approach the trigger and delay the compulsion
  4. 2 Read or briefly approach a related trigger
  5. 1 Write down the feared thought in specific words

This is only an example. Every ERP plan is individualized based on the client's symptoms, triggers, history, and clinical needs.

Obsessions & compulsions

Common OCD symptoms and patterns

OCD symptoms usually include obsessions and compulsions. Obsessions are unwanted thoughts, images, sensations, or urges that feel threatening, disturbing, or impossible to dismiss. Compulsions are behaviors or mental rituals used to reduce anxiety, prevent something bad from happening, or make things feel “right.”

Obsessions may include:

  • Fear of contamination, germs, illness, or chemicals
  • Fear of harming someone accidentally or intentionally
  • Intrusive sexual, violent, religious, or taboo thoughts
  • Doubt about whether something was done correctly
  • Fear of making a mistake or being responsible for harm
  • Need for certainty, symmetry, order, or a “just right” feeling

Compulsions may include:

  • Washing, cleaning, or sanitizing
  • Checking locks, appliances, body sensations, or messages
  • Counting, tapping, repeating, or arranging
  • Mental reviewing, silent prayer, neutralizing, or analyzing
  • Asking for reassurance from family, partners, clinicians, or the internet
  • Avoiding people, places, objects, numbers, words, or situations

Common types

Types of OCD we treat

OCD can show up in many forms, and many adults experience more than one pattern at the same time. The content of the obsession may differ, but the treatment goal is similar: reduce compulsions, reduce avoidance, and build tolerance for uncertainty.

Contamination OCD

Fear of germs, dirt, illness, or chemicals, often with washing, cleaning, or avoidance compulsions.

Checking OCD

Repeated worries about safety, harm, or mistakes, with checking of locks, appliances, the body, or messages.

Harm OCD

Unwanted intrusive thoughts of harming oneself or others, even though the person does not want to act on them.

Scrupulosity

Religious or moral obsessions, with fear of having done something wrong or sinful and compulsions such as praying or confessing.

Symmetry & “just right” OCD

A strong need for order, balance, or a “just right” feeling, with arranging, counting, or repeating compulsions.

Pure O

Primarily obsessional OCD, where compulsions happen internally through mental reviewing, reassurance-seeking, checking feelings, analyzing thoughts, or trying to neutralize intrusive thoughts.

Relationship OCD

Constant doubt and questioning about feelings, attraction, or certainty in a relationship, with checking and reassurance compulsions.

Hoarding-related concerns

Hoarding disorder is a separate diagnosis, but hoarding symptoms may appear alongside OCD or OCD-like patterns.

The cycle that follows

OCD, intrusive thoughts, and reassurance-seeking.

One of the most painful parts of OCD is that intrusive thoughts often attack what a person values most. Someone with harm OCD may be deeply distressed by thoughts of hurting someone they love. Someone with scrupulosity may fear they have done something morally wrong. Someone with relationship OCD may constantly question feelings, attraction, or certainty.

The problem is not the presence of an intrusive thought. The problem is the cycle that follows: fear, analysis, checking, avoidance, reassurance, and temporary relief. Reassurance may feel helpful in the moment, but it often teaches the brain to keep asking for certainty.

At Mental Wellness KS, clients and families learn how reassurance and accommodation can keep OCD going, and how to respond in ways that support treatment rather than strengthen the cycle.

Psychiatric support

Medication support for OCD.

Many adults with OCD benefit from medication alongside ERP therapy. Selective serotonin reuptake inhibitors, also called SSRIs, are commonly used for OCD when clinically appropriate. Some clients may require different dosing strategies or medication timelines than they experienced in treatment for other conditions.

At Mental Wellness KS, medication decisions are made individually through psychiatric evaluation and ongoing medication support. The goal is not to replace ERP, but to support the broader treatment plan when medication may help reduce symptom intensity and improve participation in therapy.

This is an overview, not prescribing advice. Medication decisions are made by the psychiatric provider, based on the client's history, symptoms, prior medication response, medical considerations, and treatment goals.

For families

Family support and reducing accommodation.

OCD often pulls families into the cycle. Loved ones may answer repeated reassurance questions, help with avoidance, change routines around triggers, or participate in rituals because it seems to reduce distress in the moment.

These responses are understandable, but over time they can make OCD stronger. Family education helps loved ones understand how OCD works, how reassurance and accommodation affect symptoms, and how to support recovery without becoming part of the ritual cycle.

At Mental Wellness KS, family support is included when clinically appropriate to help families communicate more effectively, reduce accommodation, and prepare for life after treatment.

Common questions

Frequently asked questions about OCD treatment

What is OCD?

Obsessive-compulsive disorder (OCD) is a mental health condition involving obsessions and compulsions. Obsessions are unwanted, intrusive thoughts, images, or urges that cause distress. Compulsions are behaviors or mental rituals performed to reduce that distress or prevent a feared outcome. OCD is treatable, most often with Exposure and Response Prevention (ERP) therapy and medication support when clinically appropriate.

What is the difference between OCD and anxiety?

Anxiety disorders involve excessive worry or fear in response to a perceived threat. OCD is structured differently: a specific intrusive thought, the obsession, is paired with a ritual performed to neutralize the distress, the compulsion. People with OCD almost always perform compulsions; people with generalized anxiety usually do not. Both can occur together, which is one reason accurate diagnosis matters.

Is OCD just being a perfectionist?

No. Perfectionism is a personality trait that is usually compatible with daily life. OCD is a clinical condition: intrusive thoughts that are unwanted and distressing, compulsions performed for relief rather than enjoyment, significant time spent on rituals, and real interference with work, school, or relationships. Casual use of the word OCD to describe tidiness has little to do with the clinical condition.

What is ERP therapy for OCD?

Exposure and Response Prevention (ERP) is the primary evidence-based therapy for OCD. ERP helps clients gradually and collaboratively approach feared thoughts or situations while not performing the compulsion that normally follows. Over time, this helps the brain learn that anxiety can rise and fall without rituals. ERP is structured, planned, and guided by the treatment team.

Do I need residential OCD treatment?

Residential OCD treatment may be appropriate when OCD symptoms take up hours each day, interfere with work, school, or relationships, or have not responded well to weekly outpatient ERP. Residential care provides 24/7 support, daily structure, and intensive ERP. Our admissions team can help determine whether residential treatment, PHP, or IOP is the right starting point.

Do you offer inpatient OCD treatment?

Many people use the word “inpatient” when searching for a higher level of mental health care. Mental Wellness KS offers residential OCD treatment, PHP, and IOP. If someone is in immediate danger, unable to stay safe, or requires acute medical stabilization, a hospital may be the appropriate first step. If they are medically stable but need structured OCD treatment, residential care may be a better fit.

What is Pure O?

Pure O refers to an OCD presentation where the compulsions are mainly mental rather than visible behaviors. Compulsions may happen internally through mental reviewing, reassurance-seeking, checking feelings, analyzing thoughts, or trying to neutralize intrusive thoughts. Compulsions are still present in Pure O, they are just harder to see. ERP works for Pure O the same way it works for more visible forms of OCD.

Why does reassurance make OCD worse?

Reassurance can reduce anxiety briefly, but it often strengthens the OCD cycle because the brain learns to rely on certainty from someone else instead of tolerating uncertainty. Family support helps loved ones respond with compassion without feeding the compulsion.

What medications are used to treat OCD?

Selective serotonin reuptake inhibitors, also called SSRIs, are commonly used for OCD when clinically appropriate, often alongside ERP therapy. Medication decisions are individualized and made by the psychiatric provider based on the client's history, symptoms, prior medication response, and treatment goals. This page does not provide prescribing advice.

How long does OCD treatment take?

Length of care varies by individual needs. A typical residential stay is 30 to 45 days, often followed by 6 to 12 weeks of PHP and IOP as clients step down. ERP and relapse-prevention work often continue in outpatient care afterward.

Many commercial insurance plans cover OCD treatment when it is medically necessary. Coverage varies by plan, benefits, diagnosis, and level of care. Our admissions team can verify your benefits at no cost.

Can OCD be cured?

OCD is often a long-term condition, but many people can experience meaningful improvement with ERP therapy, medication support when clinically appropriate, family education, and relapse-prevention planning. Treatment focuses on reducing compulsions, reducing avoidance, improving functioning, and helping clients respond differently to intrusive thoughts.
